Inspectivity is a SaaS platform built for commercial and industrial teams that need to move inspection and maintenance paperwork into a more structured digital workflow. Inspectivity sits in a practical, operations-heavy space, so the website needed to explain the product clearly while also showing how it could be used in real-world inspection and maintenance workflows.

## The Brief
The project was to build out a substantial marketing website for Inspectivity, a SaaS platform used in industrial inspection, maintenance, and compliance workflows. The site had already been planned around a large amount of product, industry, and resource content. My role was to help bring that direction to life and implement it within WordPress.

The main challenge was scale. This meant working across more than 80 pages of content, including product pages, industry pages, case studies, FAQs, white papers, and supporting resource pages. 

The build also involved a wide range of page sections and layout types, including media and text columns, grids, carousels, sliders, mega menus, and icon-heavy content blocks. There was a lot to build, a lot to keep consistent, and a lot of detail that needed to hold together across a large SaaS marketing site.
## My Role
I worked on the project as a developer at Workhouse Advertising, helping build out the site in WordPress using the Avada theme framework.

My role was focused on implementation rather than defining the creative direction. I worked across a large number of pages and layout types, translating the planned designs and content structure into working website sections. This included building page layouts, setting up reusable content patterns, working with custom post types, and keeping the visual details consistent across the site.

Because of the size of the project, consistency was a major part of the work. I created reusable layouts and templates that could be applied across different sections of the site, helping the build stay more manageable as the number of pages grew.

Later in the project, other developers also worked on parts of the build. The reusable patterns I had put in place helped give them a clearer structure to work within, so new pages and sections could continue to follow the same overall direction.
## The Build
Using Avada gave us the flexibility to build a large number of page layouts without needing every section to be developed from scratch. For a site of this size, that flexibility was useful, but it also meant the build needed a consistent approach behind it.

Most of the work was centred around creating and applying repeatable page sections. These included media and text blocks, feature grids, icon-heavy content sections, sliders, carousels, resource layouts, and navigation elements. Some areas of the site also used custom post types, including case studies, FAQs, white papers, and other structured content.

Because the site was so content-heavy, the build could not rely on one or two fixed templates. Different pages needed different layouts, but they still needed to feel like part of the same website. A lot of the work was in taking the planned visual direction and turning it into a set of reusable patterns that could support more than 80 pages without becoming unmanageable.

The result was less about one big technical feature and more about careful production work: building pages, reusing patterns, keeping visual details aligned, and making sure the website could handle a large amount of SaaS marketing content.
## The Challenge
Keeping a large website consistent is a challenge on its own. Keeping it consistent while still allowing pages to feel varied is where Inspectivity became more interesting.

Inspectivity was not built around one or two simple page templates. The site had a wide range of content types, page layouts, reusable sections, icon treatments, and visual patterns. Across more than 80 pages, even small inconsistencies could start to stand out.

So how do you keep a large, content-heavy website feeling consistent without making every page feel the same?

That was the real challenge. Avada gave us the flexibility to build pages quickly, but it also meant the build needed discipline. With that many pages and layout variations, it would have been very easy for the site to drift: slightly different spacing here, a different card treatment there, a section that almost matched another one but not quite.

A lot of the work came down to managing detail at scale: spacing, responsive behaviour, visual hierarchy, section structure, icon usage, and the way repeated components were applied across different parts of the site.

The challenge was not just building pages. It was making sure all those pages still felt like they belonged to the same product website.
## The Outcome
For me, Inspectivity stands out because of its scale. It was not a case of building one clever feature and calling the project done. It was a long production build with a lot of moving parts, a lot of page variation, and a lot of detail that needed to stay consistent from one section to the next.

The finished website brought together product information, industry content, case studies, FAQs, white papers, and other resources into one substantial WordPress site.

It also gave me more experience working inside a larger agency process, where the work is shared across different people and the final result depends on keeping everything aligned. My contribution was in helping build the site out properly, creating reusable patterns, and keeping the visual implementation consistent across a large amount of content.

Inspectivity may not be the most technically complex project in my portfolio, but it shows my ability to work through a large, content-heavy website and keep the final build consistent.
